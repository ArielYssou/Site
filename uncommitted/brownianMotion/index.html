Random events are present in many physical systems, and create several intriguing behaviors and patterns

But what is randomness and how we measure it? Isn't the universe fully deterministic to some degree? This question was a very import issue in physics for quite some time and play a key role in the philosophy behind our science. In the past, before the formulation and overall acceptance of quantum physics, many physicists thought nature to be fully deterministic, and all imprecisions due to technological limitations or a plain wrong theory. Image that a being (Laplace's deamon) is capable of knowing the correct position and speed of all the particles of all the universe, would it be capable of foreseeing the future (perhaps even the future for any given time)? If everything follows Newtons laws then, at first glance, it might seems that this is the case, but this does not seem to be a reasonable conclusion.

Recent (\(\pm\)100 years) developments in the fields of chaos and quantum mechanics showed us that even if you know something with extreme precision, the ramifications are just boundless and impossible to foresee with absolute accuracy. Quantum mechanics also establishes the uncertainty principle that is know even by science enthusiasts (albeit their comprehension of the concept is sketchy and/or misguided in most, if not all, cases), which puts a nail on the coffin (as a note, the well know uncertainty principle o W. Heisemberg about position and momentum is not the only uncertainty relation in nature, it is actually a particular case of a much more general result about incompatible observables, that is a direct implication of quantum dynamics formalism. See Sakurai 'Modern Quantum Mechanis' and references therein). So if nothing is truly deterministic, is classical mechanics fundamentally wrong and useless?

This is obviously not the case as it all comes down to scope. Most classical systems are not affected by quantum uncertainties and the classical mechanics formalism is quite robust and general.
